Executive Summary & AISA Policy Vision

This document establishes AISA’s institutional expectations for the strategic integration and use of artificial intelligence across teaching, learning, assessment, and operations. It is designed to do two things at once: protect students and institutional integrity, and equip the school community to use AI well.

If the policy only restricted AI, it would fail students who need to learn how to work with it. If it only enabled AI, it would fail the school’s obligations to safeguard data, uphold academic integrity, and comply with ADEK and UAE law.

Successful implementation requires the Digital Wellbeing Committee to strategically and ethically embed AI. This ensures AI adoption is deliberate, safe, and educationally valuable, upholding AISA's Strategic Pillar 3 (Future-Ready Learning) which commits the school to preparing students for a world where AI is embedded in professional, academic, and civic life.

The AI Opportunity for AISA

Schools that adopt AI without governance structures tend to get stuck: tools proliferate without oversight, assessment integrity erodes quietly, and data protection becomes a retrospective problem. Research on organizational AI adoption consistently shows that institutions without formal governance frameworks experience higher rates of misuse, inconsistent practice, and compliance failures.

At the same time, schools that over-restrict AI risk producing graduates who are less prepared than their peers for university and employment. The goal of this plan is not to slow AI adoption. It is to make it deliberate, safe, and educationally valuable for the American International School in Abu Dhabi.

AI Tool Approval & Procurement

No AI tool may be used for school-related purposes involving school data unless it appears on the AISA Approved Tools Register. The approval process targets a maximum of 15 school days.

  • 1
    Request (Day 0): Staff submit proposal to IT / Data Protection Lead via the AI Tool Request Form.
  • 2
    Technical Review (Days 1-7): IT Lead assesses data protection, security, and safeguarding criteria.
  • 3
    Pedagogical Review (Days 5-10): Head of Innovation evaluates instructional value, curriculum alignment, and assessment implications.
  • 4
    Decision (Days 10-15): School Director (or Committee for complex cases) approves, conditionally approves (Pilot Status), or declines.
  • 5
    Registration (Within 2 days): Added to Approved Tools Register.

Data Protection in Practice: Quick Decision Aid

AI tool use at AISA must comply with UAE Federal Decree-Law No. 45 of 2021. No student personal data may be entered into unapproved tools. Below is a practical guide for staff:

What You Want to Do Data Involved Rule
Analyze assessment patterns Student names + scores Approved tool only, approved for assessment data
Analyze assessment patterns De-identified scores (no names, no small-group identifiers) Any approved tool
Draft a lesson plan No student data Any approved tool
Draft a parent email about a student Student name + sensitive details Remove name/details from AI input; add back manually
Upload a class photo for an AI task Student images (personal data) Not permitted unless specifically approved

Guiding Principles & Ethical Framework

These six principles are not aspirational statements. They are the criteria against which all AI-related decisions, tool approvals, and practices will be evaluated at AISA. Each principle carries equal weight.

Principle 1: Human-Centered Learning

AI augments teaching and supports learning. It does not replace the professional judgement of teachers, the thinking of students, or the relational core of education. Every AI application must demonstrably serve student learning or operational effectiveness while preserving human agency in educational decisions.

Principle 2: Ethics and Fairness

AI use at AISA must be fair, transparent, and accountable. This means actively identifying and mitigating bias in AI outputs, ensuring equitable access across student populations, and never using AI to make consequential decisions about students without human oversight. This aligns with the UAE Charter for the Development and Use of Artificial Intelligence.

Principle 3: Academic Integrity

AI must not undermine the validity of student assessment or the development of independent thinking. Students must be taught to use AI as a learning tool, not a substitute for intellectual effort. This reflects the IB’s requirement that AI-generated content in submitted work must be acknowledged and referenced.

Principle 4: Data Protection and Privacy

Student and staff data are protected under UAE Federal Decree-Law No. 45 of 2021. No AI tool may process personal data without lawful basis, appropriate consent, and compliance with AISA’s data protection obligations.

Principle 5: Safeguarding

AI tools used with or by students must meet safeguarding standards, including age-appropriate access controls, content filtering, and protection from harmful, manipulative, or exploitative AI-generated content. Student wellbeing takes precedence over any operational or instructional benefit.

Principle 6: Professional Accountability

Staff are professionally accountable for how they use AI. Using AI does not diminish a teacher’s responsibility for the quality, accuracy, and appropriateness of their professional work. Accountability also means modelling: if AISA expects students to engage in deep thinking, staff must demonstrate the same standard. AI should amplify professional thinking, not replace it.

Personal AI Accounts

AISA recognises that many staff hold personal subscriptions to AI tools (e.g., personal ChatGPT). Using a personal AI account for school-adjacent thinking, brainstorming, or professional learning is not prohibited, provided that no student-identifiable data, staff-identifiable data, or confidential school information is entered. The line is this: if the AI tool touches school data, it must be approved.

Teacher Professional Development & Assessment Frameworks

The Professional Standards Conversation (A Note on Workload)

AI does not automatically reduce workload. As research notes (Ranganathan & Ye, 2026), AI adoption can intensify work by raising expectations for output quality. Furthermore, efficiency is not the same as effectiveness. The question for every professional at AISA is: where does AI amplify my thinking, and where does it replace it? Our PD starts here, ensuring AI compliance is low-friction and that assessment redesign is supported with collaborative planning time.

Phase 1: Understanding Needs & Readiness

PD begins with deploying comprehensive readiness surveys to assess current AI usage, comfort levels, and perceived ethical challenges. The aggregated results directly inform the prioritization of training modules.

Phase 2: Foundational AI Literacy & Ethics

  • Demystifying AI: Core concepts, LLMs, and AI's impact on K-12 education.
  • Ethical Application: Deep dives into Data Privacy (UAE laws, the Quick Data Decision Aid), Algorithmic Bias, and Academic Integrity.
  • The 5-Step Scaffolding Scale: Training teachers on how to use this scale to set clear expectations for student assignments.
The 5-Step Scaffolding Scale: Setting Expectations
Level Label What Students May/Must Do
1 No AI Assistance Students rely solely on their own knowledge. No AI tools are used at any point.
2 AI-Assisted Brainstorming Use AI to generate ideas. Final content must be created without direct AI input. AI must be cited.
3 AI-Supported Drafting AI can help with initial drafts. The final version must be significantly revised by the student.
4 AI-Collaborative Creation AI-generated content included. Students critically evaluate and edit. AI usage transparently cited.
5 AI as Co-Creator Extensive use of AI. Students provide a rationale for AI use and ensure original thought.

Phase 3: Practical Integration & Frameworks

Hands-on training with specific AISA Approved Tools (e.g., MagicSchool, NotebookLM) and the pedagogical frameworks that guide their use:

H-AI-H (Human Inquiry → AI → Human Empowerment)

Every AI interaction should begin with the student's own inquiry, use AI as a productive tool in the middle, and conclude with human reflection, editing, and demonstrated understanding.

The SHIFT Framework for Critical Evaluation
  • S: Start your curiosity engine (What intrigues me?)
  • H: Hone in on a detail (What did AI get right/wrong?)
  • I: Identify your context (How does this fit my work?)
  • F: Frame it from a new perspective
  • T: Talk about what's missing (Limitations?)

Phase 4: Ongoing Support & Community of Practice

  • Dedicated Support: Virtual/in-person office hours with the Head of Innovation.
  • "AI Innovators" CoP: Establishing a voluntary group for passionate teachers to share best practices, pilot new tools, and lead peer-to-peer learning.
  • Subject-Specific Workshops: Advanced modules focusing on advanced prompt engineering and assessment redesign (e.g., shifting toward oral defenses and process-oriented projects).

Grade 3 AI-Integrated Transdisciplinary PBL Example

This sample Project-Based Learning (PBL) unit demonstrates how Artificial Intelligence can be woven into a Grade 3 curriculum. The focus is on authentic learning experiences and the H-AI-H framework, utilizing only tools from the Approved Register.

PBL Unit: Local Ecosystem Engineers!

Driving Question: How can we, as young scientists, use technology to investigate and share the amazing ways local plants and animals survive in Abu Dhabi?

Project Goal: Students research a local plant or animal (e.g., Arabian Oryx, Ghaf tree) and create a multimedia presentation using approved AI tools to support research and creation.

Subject Integration & AI Connections:

Language Arts
  • Information Synthesis (Level 2): Teacher uploads curated articles into Google NotebookLM. Students ask questions to the source document to deepen understanding safely.
  • Teacher Prep: Teacher utilizes Diffit or MagicSchool Enterprise to instantly generate differentiated reading passages on the subject for varying EAL levels.
Mathematics
  • Data Analysis: AI tools help create simple charts from inputted data about animal habitats.
  • Teacher Prep: Teachers use MagicSchool to generate differentiated word problems related to the ecosystem theme.
Science
  • "What if" Scenarios (Level 2): With teacher guidance via MagicSchool Student Rooms, the class explores cause-and-effect scenarios (e.g., "What happens if there is less rainfall?").
  • Presentation Design: Students use Canva for Education, utilizing its built-in Magic Design suggestions.

Authentic Assessment Strategies:

Assessment focuses on both process and product, adhering to Principle 3 (Academic Integrity).

  • Oral Defense/Presentation Q&A: Students answer questions about their research process, allowing teachers to gauge genuine understanding.
  • Research Journal (Process): Students maintain a journal noting their drafts, reflections, and how they used AI tools (including refining prompts).
  • Multimedia Presentation: Assessed via a rubric (generated via Brisk Teaching or MagicSchool) focusing on scientific accuracy and appropriate use of AI tools (Digital Citizenship).
  • AI Disclosure: Students practice noting where Canva Magic Design or NotebookLM assisted their work.

Measuring Impact & Continuous Improvement

A commitment to data-driven decision-making is central to AISA's strategy, monitored by the Digital Wellbeing Committee.

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

Teacher & Tech Impact
  • Approved Tool Analytics: High, sustained adoption rates indicated by platform enterprise analytics (MagicSchool, Google Workspace, Canva).
  • Approval SLA: IT/Data Protection Lead meeting the 15-day turnaround target for new tool requests.
  • Teacher-reported increases in efficiency for tasks like lesson planning and differentiation.
  • Reduction in unauthorized/unapproved tool usage.
Student Outcomes
  • Consistent student application of the SHIFT framework when evaluating sources.
  • Proper utilization of AI Disclosures based on the 5-Step Scaffolding Scale set by teachers.
  • Quality and depth of research in capstone projects (like the IB Extended Essay) using safe AI tools.
  • Demonstrable improvement in student engagement in AI-integrated lessons.
School-Level Impact
  • Successful integration of AI literacy into the AISA curriculum, aligning with UAE MoE mandates.
  • Recognition of AISA as a leader in innovative and responsible AI integration in education.
  • Positive feedback from parents and the community on AISA's forward-thinking approach.

Data Collection & Analysis Framework

A mixed-methods approach will be employed for ongoing data collection:

  • Quantitative Data: Surveys, usage analytics from Approved Platforms (where ethical), and participation rates in PD.
  • Qualitative Data: Focus groups, interviews with teachers and students, classroom observations, and feedback from the Digital Wellbeing Committee.
  • Reporting: Regular reports on KPIs and progress will be shared with school leadership to inform strategic decisions.

Iterative Improvement Cycle

AISA will adopt a continuous improvement model for its AI strategy:

  1. Plan: Define goals and strategies for AI integration.
  2. Do: Implement AI initiatives, including PD, tool deployment, and curriculum integration.
  3. Check: Collect and analyze data on KPIs and monitor the ethical implications of AI use.
  4. Act: Based on the analysis, refine strategies, update policies via the Committee, adjust PD programs, or scale successful initiatives.
